The Problem

Custom orthotics are still made by hand.

Clinicians spend hours on manual casting, foam-box impressions, and subjective assessments. Each patient requires an entirely new workflow — time-consuming, inconsistent, and dependent on practitioner experience.

The result: costly delays, variable outcomes, and clinical staff stretched thin doing data entry instead of delivering care. In a world of precision medicine, orthotic design is stuck in the past.

Business Model

Built for scale.
Designed for clinics.

We evaluated three go-to-market approaches and selected the hybrid model as the optimal balance between clinical adoption, revenue scalability, and operational complexity.

Option 1

SaaS Only

Clinics subscribe to our design software and manage their own fabrication workflow independently.

  • Recurring, predictable revenue
  • High feasibility, low complexity
  • Weak differentiation from competitors
  • Lower revenue ceiling
Option 2

Full Service

End-to-end turnkey solution: data capture, design, manufacturing, and logistics all managed by us.

  • Strong differentiation
  • High full value-chain revenue
  • Complex logistics and scaling
  • High operational risk
✓ Chosen Option 3 — Hybrid

SaaS + Fabrication

Platform subscription for design tools, with optional fabrication through our distributed production network.

  • Flexible adoption for clinics
  • Recurring + transactional revenue
  • Scalable through distributed network
  • Moderate, manageable risk

Risk & Mitigation

The real risk is adoption,
not technology.

Likelihood: Medium Impact: High

Demand Risk

Clinics may not adopt the platform if behavior change friction outweighs perceived efficiency gains.

Mitigation

Pilot programs with early adopter clinics to demonstrate ROI
Co-design process with clinical partners from day one
Beachhead: target clinics already generating custom orthotic revenue
Likelihood: Medium Impact: Medium

Technology Risk

ML automation may not deliver sufficient clinical value in early versions, undermining trust.

Mitigation

Start with simple, transparent workflows before complex automation
Incremental ML updates validated with clinical feedback loops
Decision-support positioning — clinician remains in control
Likelihood: Low–Med Impact: High

Regulatory Risk

Medical device classification could impose regulatory barriers if the platform is positioned as a diagnostic tool.

Mitigation

Design-support positioning — avoid diagnostic claims
Early engagement with regulatory advisors
Clinical decision remains with licensed practitioner

Critical insight: The primary barrier to market entry is behavior change, not technology. Competitors face the same barrier — giving first movers who build clinical credibility a durable advantage.
